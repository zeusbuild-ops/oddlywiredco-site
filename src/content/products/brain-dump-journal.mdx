There is a kind of ADHD overwhelm that nobody really warns you about. It's not anxiety, not depression, not procrastination — though it gets mistaken for all three. It's just that your brain has loaded twenty-six things into working memory at the same time and now none of them can move.

The work email. The text you meant to send three days ago. The thing you were excited about an hour ago and have since completely forgotten. The thing you keep noticing on the ceiling. The thing your mum said in 2014. The thing you should have said in the meeting. The thing.

The brain dump isn't a productivity hack. It's a release valve. You don't need to organise the contents. You don't need them to be coherent. You just need them out, on a page, where they can stop competing for the same three neurons.

This journal is hardback (because soft covers feel apologetic). It's small enough to live next to you. It opens flat. The pages are blank — no prompts, no productivity matrix, no "what are you grateful for today." Just space.

Use it badly. Use it in the wrong order. Use it for shopping lists and intrusive thoughts and song lyrics. That's the point.
